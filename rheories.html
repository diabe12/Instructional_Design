Behaviourism

Behaviourism

Behaviourism is a learning theory that focuses on observable behaviours and how they are influenced by environmental stimuli. The theory suggests that learning occurs when individuals respond to stimuli in their environment and that these responses can be strengthened or weakened through reinforcement and punishment. Behaviourists believe that internal mental processes are less important than observable actions when studying learning.

According to behaviourism, learning is a change in behaviour that results from experience. Positive reinforcement, such as rewards and praise, can encourage desired behaviours, while punishment may discourage undesirable behaviours. Learners are generally viewed as passive recipients of information, and teachers play a central role in controlling the learning environment and providing appropriate reinforcement.

Major contributors to behaviourism include B.F. Skinner, who developed the concept of operant conditioning, and John B. Watson, who is widely regarded as the founder of behaviourism. Their work has influenced educational practices such as programmed instruction, drill-and-practice exercises, classroom management techniques, and the use of rewards to motivate learners.

In instructional technology, behaviourism supports the use of computer-assisted instruction, educational software, quizzes, and immediate feedback systems. These tools help reinforce learning by providing learners with opportunities to practice skills and receive prompt feedback on their performance.

References

Skinner, B.F. (1953). Science and Human Behavior. New York: Macmillan.

Watson, J.B. (1913). Psychology as the Behaviorist Views It. Psychological Review, 20(2), pp.158–177.

Cognitivism

Cognitivism is a learning theory that focuses on the mental processes involved in learning. Unlike behaviourism, which emphasizes observable behaviours, cognitivism examines how learners acquire, process, store, and retrieve information. The theory views the mind as an information-processing system and suggests that learning occurs when knowledge is organized and integrated into existing cognitive structures.

According to cognitivists, learning is influenced by factors such as perception, memory, attention, problem-solving, and understanding. Learners are considered active participants in the learning process because they interpret and organize information rather than simply responding to external stimuli. Teachers therefore play the role of facilitators who help learners connect new information to prior knowledge and develop meaningful understanding.

One of the major contributors to cognitivism is Jean Piaget, who proposed that cognitive development occurs through stages and that learners construct knowledge through interaction with their environment. Cognitivism has significantly influenced educational practices, including the use of advance organizers, concept mapping, and instructional strategies that promote critical thinking and problem-solving.

In instructional technology, cognitivism supports the design of learning materials that enhance information processing, memory retention, and knowledge organization. This approach helps learners develop deeper understanding and transfer knowledge to new situations.

References

Piaget, J. (1952). The Origins of Intelligence in Children. New York: International Universities Press.

Schunk, D.H. (2020). Learning Theories: An Educational Perspective. 8th edn. Boston: Pearson.

Constructivism

Constructivism is a learning theory that emphasizes that learners actively construct their own knowledge and understanding through experiences and interactions with their environment. Rather than passively receiving information, learners build new knowledge by connecting it to their existing knowledge, beliefs, and experiences. The theory suggests that learning is a personal and meaningful process in which individuals create their own understanding of the world.

According to constructivists, learning occurs when learners engage in activities that require exploration, discovery, problem-solving, and critical thinking. Learners are viewed as active participants in the learning process, while teachers act as facilitators who guide, support, and provide opportunities for meaningful learning experiences. Collaboration and social interaction are also considered important because learners can develop deeper understanding through discussion and shared experiences.

Major contributors to constructivism include Jean Piaget, who emphasized cognitive development and individual knowledge construction, and Lev Vygotsky, who highlighted the importance of social interaction and cultural influences on learning. Their ideas have greatly influenced modern educational practices, including project-based learning, collaborative learning, inquiry-based learning, and problem-based learning.

In instructional technology, constructivism supports the use of interactive learning environments, simulations, multimedia resources, and collaborative tools that allow learners to actively engage with content and construct their own understanding. This approach helps learners develop critical thinking skills, creativity, and the ability to apply knowledge in real-world situations.

References

Piaget, J. (1954). The Construction of Reality in the Child. New York: Basic Books.

Vygotsky, L.S. (1978). Mind in Society: The Development of Higher Psychological Processes. Cambridge, MA: Harvard University Press.
