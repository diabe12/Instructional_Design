Instructional Design Models

ADDIE Model

The ADDIE Model is a systematic instructional design framework used for developing effective educational and training programs. The acronym ADDIE stands for Analysis, Design, Development, Implementation, and Evaluation. The model provides a structured approach to creating learning experiences that meet the needs of learners and achieve specific learning objectives.

The first phase, Analysis, involves identifying the learning problem, determining the needs of learners, and establishing instructional goals. During the Design phase, learning objectives, assessment methods, instructional strategies, and course structures are planned. The Development phase focuses on creating and assembling learning materials, resources, and activities based on the design specifications.

In the Implementation phase, the instructional program is delivered to learners, and instructors facilitate the learning process. The final phase, Evaluation, involves assessing the effectiveness of the instruction and determining whether the learning objectives have been achieved. Evaluation may be conducted throughout the process as formative evaluation and at the end as summative evaluation.

The ADDIE Model is widely used in instructional technology because it promotes organized planning, efficient resource use, and continuous improvement. It helps instructional designers create learner-centered educational experiences that enhance knowledge acquisition, skill development, and overall learning outcomes.

References

Branch, R.M. (2009). Instructional Design: The ADDIE Approach. New York: Springer.

Molenda, M. (2015). In Search of the Elusive ADDIE Model. Performance Improvement, 54(2), pp.40–42.

ASSURE Model

The ASSURE Model is an instructional design framework that helps teachers and instructional designers effectively integrate technology and media into teaching and learning. The model is learner-centered and provides a systematic process for planning, delivering, and evaluating instruction. ASSURE is an acronym that stands for Analyze Learners, State Objectives, Select Methods, Media and Materials, Utilize Media and Materials, Require Learner Participation, and Evaluate and Revise.

The first step, Analyze Learners, involves identifying the characteristics, needs, abilities, and learning styles of students. In the State Objectives phase, clear and measurable learning objectives are developed to guide instruction. The Select Methods, Media and Materials phase focuses on choosing appropriate teaching strategies, instructional materials, and technological tools that support the learning objectives.

During the Utilize Media and Materials phase, the selected resources are prepared and used effectively in the learning environment. The Require Learner Participation phase encourages active involvement of learners through discussions, activities, practice exercises, and collaborative tasks. Active participation helps learners construct knowledge and retain information more effectively.

The final step, Evaluate and Revise, involves assessing learner performance, evaluating the effectiveness of instructional materials and methods, and making necessary improvements. The ASSURE Model is widely used in instructional technology because it promotes effective integration of technology, active learning, and continuous improvement of instructional practices.

Dick and Carey Model

The Dick and Carey Model is a systematic instructional design model developed by Walter Dick and Lou Carey. It provides a step-by-step approach to designing, developing, implementing, and evaluating effective instructional materials. The model views instruction as a complete system in which all components, including learners, instructors, materials, objectives, and assessments, work together to achieve desired learning outcomes.

The model consists of several stages, including identifying instructional goals, conducting instructional analysis, analyzing learners and learning contexts, writing performance objectives, developing assessment instruments, designing instructional strategies, developing and selecting instructional materials, conducting formative evaluation, revising instruction, and conducting summative evaluation. Each stage contributes to improving the quality and effectiveness of instruction.

A major strength of the Dick and Carey Model is its emphasis on continuous evaluation and revision throughout the instructional design process. Formative evaluation helps identify areas that need improvement before the instruction is fully implemented, while summative evaluation measures the overall success of the instructional program after implementation.

In instructional technology, the Dick and Carey Model is widely used to develop classroom lessons, online courses, training programs, and multimedia learning materials. The model ensures that instructional content, teaching strategies, assessments, and learning objectives are well aligned, leading to effective teaching and improved learner performance.

References

Dick, W., Carey, L. and Carey, J.O. (2015). The Systematic Design of Instruction. 8th edn. Boston: Pearson.

Dick, W. and Carey, L. (1978). The Systematic Design of Instruction. Glenview, IL: Scott, Foresman and Company.

Kemp Model

The Kemp Model is an instructional design model developed by Jerrold E. Kemp. It provides a flexible and learner-centered approach to designing instructional programs. Unlike linear models, the Kemp Model allows instructional designers to begin at any stage of the design process and make adjustments as needed. The model emphasizes the interdependence of all elements involved in instruction and focuses on meeting learners' needs effectively.

The Kemp Model consists of several key components, including identifying instructional problems, analyzing learner characteristics, defining learning objectives, selecting instructional content, designing learning activities, choosing appropriate instructional resources, and developing evaluation strategies. These components work together to ensure that instruction is effective and aligned with educational goals.

A major strength of the Kemp Model is its flexibility. Instructional designers can revisit and modify different elements of the design process whenever necessary. This makes the model suitable for a wide range of educational settings, including classroom instruction, online learning, and corporate training programs.

In instructional technology, the Kemp Model supports the creation of learner-centered learning environments by emphasizing careful planning, resource selection, and continuous evaluation. The model helps educators develop instructional materials that promote meaningful learning and improve learner performance.

References

Kemp, J.E., Morrison, G.R. and Ross, S.M. (1998). Designing Effective Instruction. 2nd edn. Upper Saddle River, NJ: Prentice Hall.

Morrison, G.R., Ross, S.M. and Kemp, J.E. (2007). Designing Effective Instruction. 5th edn. Hoboken, NJ: John Wiley & Sons.

Gagné's Nine Events of Instruction

Gagné's Nine Events of Instruction is an instructional design model developed by Robert M. Gagné. The model provides a systematic sequence of teaching activities that support effective learning. It is based on the idea that specific instructional events correspond to and facilitate the cognitive processes involved in learning. The model is widely used in education, training, and instructional technology to improve learner engagement and achievement.

The nine events include: (1) Gaining learners' attention, (2) Informing learners of the objectives, (3) Stimulating recall of prior learning, (4) Presenting the content, (5) Providing learning guidance, (6) Eliciting performance or practice, (7) Providing feedback, (8) Assessing performance, and (9) Enhancing retention and transfer of learning. These steps help learners move from initial attention to long-term retention and application of knowledge.

According to Gagné, effective instruction should be carefully structured to guide learners through each stage of the learning process. The model encourages active participation, continuous feedback, and opportunities for practice, which contribute to better understanding and improved performance.

In instructional technology, Gagné's Nine Events of Instruction are used to design lessons, e-learning courses, multimedia materials, and training programs. The model helps educators organize instructional activities in a way that promotes meaningful learning, knowledge retention, and the transfer of skills to real-life situations.

References

Gagné, R.M., Wager, W.W., Golas, K.C. and Keller, J.M. (2005). Principles of Instructional Design. 5th edn. Belmont, CA: Wadsworth.

Gagné, R.M. (1985). The Conditions of Learning and Theory of Instruction. 4th edn. New York: Holt, Rinehart and Winston.
